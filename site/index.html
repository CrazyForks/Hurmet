Approximations of Pi

Over time, we have increased the accuracy of our approximations of . The ancients used 3 (Bablylon) or 3.16 (ancient Egypt).

Archimedes, in 250 BC, invented the polygon method: Take a polygon. Then take a slightly larger polygon. One can inscribe a circle between the two polygons. You can calculate the perimeter of each polygon via the Pythagorean theorem. Then you know that the perimeter of the circle is between those two values.

The polygon method was the state of the art for 1900 years. Archimedes himself applied the method to a 96-sided polygon and found . Others gradually increased the accuracy until Ludolph van Ceulen reached 30 significant digits in 1615.

After Newton and Leibnitz invented calculus, the polygon method was augmented by the Taylor series for arctangent at 1: That series can be continued to any required accuracy and was used with hand-calculations to gradually increase accuracy up to 808 decimal places in 1947.

Then computer calculations and elliptic functions took over. The current record is 50,000,000,000,000 digits.
