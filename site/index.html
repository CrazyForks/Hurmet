This week’s example:

Cube-Quad Effect

Last week, I mentioned that ancient builders, before building a structure, would often build a small scale model. Scale models are helpful, but they will not accurately predict full-size performance. For instance, columns have a square-cube effect. When you scale up a structure, a column’s cross-sectional area goes up by the square of the scale factor, but the weight of a floor goes up by the cube of the scale factor.

Ancient beams performed worse than columns. Instead of a square-cube effect, beams have a cube-quad effect. Say that a wooden beam has a rectangular cross section. It’s ability to resisting bending will increase with the cube of the scale factor.

The problem is that beam bending will increase quadractically with the scale factor.

Configurations like domes or arches can be built so that all forces are compressive and their square-cube inaccuracy could be overcome by the very high compressive strengths of materials like masonry or stone.

The ancients had no materials available with really high tension strength. They did not have large reserves of bending capacity, so it was important to model bending accurately. The cube-quad effect prevented them from doing so.
