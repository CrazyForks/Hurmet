This week’s example:

Thrust Line

Ancient builders created many long-lasting monumental structures, but only of certain types. Ancient builders did not know modern principles of strength of materials, but they could create a small scale model before building the final structure. Scale models told them part of the story, but not all of it.

Build a small scale model; then build a large full scale structure. The area of a column cross-section goes up by the square of the scale factor:

But the weight of a floor goes up by the cube of the scale factor:

Architecture books call this the square-cube law. This is a problem for scale models. The final column stresses will be much higher than what a scale model will represent.

But the square-cube effect in columns was not the biggest problem for ancient builders. As long as columns were not slender and the column was in compression, not bending, the builders were okay. Many building materials, like masonry or stone, are really, really good at resisting compression.

Consider a dome or an arch. If one were to cut a cross-section at all points, and calculate all the compressive forces acting on that section, and calculate the centroid of where those forces occur, the results form a thrust line. If you keep the thrust line within the middle third of the cross-section, then the structure sees only compression forces, not bending.

Or instead of doing calculations, you could build a scale model. A scale model will accurately tell you if that thrust line is inside the middle third.

This explains why ancient domes and arches have survived intact to the present day. With scale models, builders could ensure that their material would undergo compression, not bending. It didn’t matter if they mis-calculated the amount of compression. Materials like masonry or stone had plenty of compressive capacity.

Beams were a problem. A beam spanning horizontally will see bending, not compression. To resist bending, each beam cross-section sees compressive stresses (yay) at the top of the cross-section and tension stresses (boo) at the bottom of the cross section.

Some ancient materials, like wood, could resist tension. But their tension capacity is moderate, not the extravagant compressive capacity of masonry. When faced with tension, it is important to get an accurate estimate of the amount of stress. You don’t have huge reserves of tension capacity.

In fact, a scale model gives worse information regarding beam bending than just a square-cube effect. Next week, we’ll discuss why.
