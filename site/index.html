Naval Architecture

Bartosz Ciechanowski has posted another of his wonderful, interactive, animated lessons. This one is an introduction to naval architecture (https://ciechanow.ski/naval-architecture). At one point, he describes and animates a ship’s metacenter. That’s an important parameter in ship design. If a ship’s metacentric distance is negative, the ship will capsize.

He does not show the calculation for a metacentric distance, but it isn’t difficult. Let’s do one.

First, some definitions. The sketch shows a raft cross section. The center of gravity is point . The displaced volume of water provides buoyancy and the center of buoyancy is point . If the ship heels (rolls to the side), then and are no are longer on the same vertical line and the difference is a moment arm which acts to right the ship.

The buoyancy force acts upward through the center of buoyancy and the metacenter, is at the intersection between the original buoyancy line of action and heeled buoyancy line of action. The metacentric distance, , is the distance from point to point . If is ever negative, the ship will capsize.

We’ll make our calculation easy and work on a rectangular plan — a raft.

Reference for this calculation is Gaythwaite, Design of Marine Facilities for Berthing, Mooring, and Repair of Vessels., Chapter 9 Design of Floating Structures.
