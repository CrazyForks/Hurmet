Euler Buckling

A structural column must resist compression from the loads placed on it. The obvious failure mechanism is the one where the compressive stress reaches the failure stress of the material. In the case of steel, that would be the stress where the steel starts to yield.

Say that we have a 40-foot tall steel W8X24 column. A W8X24 is a standard I-section that is 8 inches deep and weighs 24 lbf per foot.

Or are we?

Short columns do indeed fail by yielding, but long, slender columns have a different failure mechanism. It’s not a strength failure; it’s a stability failure. It’s buckling.

If a column is not quite perfectly straight, it undergoes something more than just compression. It also undergoes bending, The bending moment is equal to the load, P, times the sideways out of plane distance. That bending causes further sideways displacement, .

In stable columns, that series adds a smaller amount with every iteration and the total is a finite amount. That’s good.

In a really slender column, that series does not terminate at a finite amount and the column buckles.
